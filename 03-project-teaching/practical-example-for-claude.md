**[01:15-02:30] WHAT IS SPME?**

[Visual: Title slide "What is SPME?"]

"SPME stands for Solid Phase Microextraction - and yes, that's a mouthful, but let's break it down.

[Visual: Animate each word]

SOLID - because we're using a solid coating, not a liquid solvent.
PHASE - the coating acts as a separate phase where analytes can partition.
MICRO - we're talking about really small amounts of coating - microliters.
EXTRACTION - we're extracting our target compounds from the sample.

[Visual: Simple diagram showing SPME principle]

Now, here's the genius of it: SPME combines extraction and concentration into ONE step. Traditional methods require multiple steps - extraction, then concentration, then cleanup. SPME does it all simultaneously.

[Visual: Comparison table]

Traditional: Extract ‚Üí Concentrate ‚Üí Cleanup ‚Üí Inject
SPME: Extract/Concentrate ‚Üí Inject

That's it. Two steps instead of four.

This technique was invented in 1989 by Professor Janusz Pawliszyn at the University of Waterloo - and yes, I had the privilege of doing my PhD in his lab, so I might be a little biased, but the numbers speak for themselves: over 10,000 research papers have used SPME since then.

It clearly works."

**[02:30-04:30] THE BASIC PRINCIPLE**

[Visual: Animated diagram of SPME extraction process]

"Okay, so how does SPME actually work? Let's understand the principle.

Imagine you have a sample - could be water, blood, food extract - containing the compounds you want to analyze. These compounds are in a complex mixture with lots of other stuff you DON'T care about.

[Visual: Highlight SPME fiber being inserted into sample]

When you insert an SPME fiber into this sample, something interesting happens. The fiber is coated with a special material - we'll talk about different coatings in Part 2 - and your target compounds start to partition from the sample matrix into this coating.

[Visual: Molecules moving from sample to coating]

Why do they partition? Because of thermodynamics. Every compound has a natural tendency to distribute itself between two phases. Some compounds love the coating more than the sample - they have high partition coefficients. Others don't - they have low partition coefficients.

This is KEY: SPME is an equilibrium technique. We're not extracting 100% of the analyte - we're extracting a predictable, reproducible amount based on this partition coefficient.

[Visual: Graph showing equilibrium curve]

As extraction time increases, more analyte moves into the coating until we reach equilibrium. At equilibrium, the amount in the coating is constant and directly related to the concentration in the sample. That's what lets us quantify.

[Visual: Show desorption step]

Once extraction is complete, we remove the fiber from the sample and insert it into our analytical instrument - usually a GC or LC-MS. Heat or solvent desorbs the analytes from the coating directly into the instrument.

[Pause]

No sample cleanup. No solvent evaporation. No waste.

That's the beauty of SPME."

---

**[04:30-06:30] SPME DEVICE COMPONENTS**

[Visual: Exploded diagram of SPME device]

"Now let's look at the actual device. An SPME unit has four main components:

[Visual: Highlight each component as described]

FIRST: The fiber. This is a thin fused silica rod - about 1 centimeter long and 100 micrometers in diameter - coated with the extraction phase. This is where the magic happens. Different coatings are available for different types of analytes - we'll cover that in Part 2.

SECOND: The needle. The fiber is housed inside a steel needle for protection. When you're not extracting, the fiber is retracted inside this needle. During extraction, you push the plunger to expose the fiber.

THIRD: The plunger. This controls fiber movement - up to retract, down to expose. Simple mechanical action.

FOURTH: The holder. This is the handle that holds everything together and provides an attachment point for autosamplers in automated systems.

[Visual: Show SPME in action - animation]

Here's how you use it in practice:

Step 1: Pierce your sample vial with the needle - fiber still retracted inside.
Step 2: Push the plunger to expose the fiber to the sample.
Step 3: Wait for extraction time - could be 5 minutes, could be 60, depends on your method.
Step 4: Retract the fiber back into the needle for protection.
Step 5: Pierce the GC or LC inlet.
Step 6: Expose the fiber again - analytes desorb into the instrument.
Step 7: Retract and you're done.

[Visual: Show cost comparison]

A single SPME fiber costs about $80-150 and can be used for hundreds to thousands of extractions. Compare that to the cost of liters of HPLC-grade solvents over time - SPME pays for itself quickly."

**[08:30-10:00] TWO MODES OF SPME**

[Visual: Side-by-side comparison diagrams]

"Before we wrap up, I want to briefly mention that SPME can be used in two different modes - and which one you choose depends on your sample type.

[Visual: Direct immersion diagram]

MODE 1: Direct Immersion SPME
The fiber is directly immersed in the liquid sample. This gives you the highest sensitivity because the fiber is in direct contact with the analytes. Use this for:
- Water samples
- Biological fluids (blood, urine) - with proper dilution
- Any liquid that won't damage the fiber coating

[Visual: Headspace diagram]

MODE 2: Headspace SPME
The fiber is exposed to the gas phase above the sample - the headspace. Analytes must vaporize from the sample before being extracted. This protects the fiber from dirty matrices and is perfect for:
- Volatile compounds
- Soil samples  
- Food samples
- Any dirty matrix that might foul the coating

[Visual: Application examples]

Real-world example: When I analyze water samples for pesticides, I use direct immersion - the water is clean, and I want maximum sensitivity.

When I analyze blood samples for drugs, I use headspace - this protects my fiber from proteins and other matrix components that would destroy it.

You'll learn how to choose between these modes as we progress through this course."
