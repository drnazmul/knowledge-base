## üìã MANUSCRIPT STRUCTURE

### **Standard IMRAD Format:**

1. **Title** (10-15 words)
2. **Abstract** (150-250 words)
3. **Introduction** (2-3 pages)
4. **Materials and Methods** (2-4 pages)
5. **Results and Discussion** (4-8 pages)
6. **Conclusions** (0.5-1 page)
7. **Acknowledgments**
8. **References**
9. **Supporting Information**

## üìù SECTION-BY-SECTION GUIDE

### **1. TITLE**

**Purpose:** Attract readers, optimize for search

**Best Practices:**
- Specific, not generic ("NADES-based SPME for..." not "New extraction method")
- Include key terms (NADES, SPME, application, analyte)
- Active voice when possible
- Avoid abbreviations (spell out in title)
- 10-15 words ideal

**Your NADES-SPME Paper Examples:**

**Good:**
"Natural Deep Eutectic Solvent-Coated Solid Phase Microextraction for Determination of Pharmaceutical Residues in Water"

**Better:**
"Choline Chloride-Glucose Deep Eutectic Solvent as Green SPME Coating for Trace Pharmaceutical Analysis in Environmental Waters"

**Why Better:**
- Specific NADES components named
- Green aspect highlighted
- Trace analysis emphasized
- Environmental application clear

---

### **2. ABSTRACT**

**Structure (150-250 words):**

1. **Background/Gap** (2-3 sentences)
   - What's the problem?
   - Why does it matter?

2. **Objective** (1 sentence)
   - What did you do?

3. **Methods** (2-3 sentences)
   - How did you do it?
   - Key innovation?

4. **Results** (3-4 sentences)
   - What did you find?
   - KEY NUMBERS (LOD, recovery, precision, etc.)

5. **Conclusions/Impact** (1-2 sentences)
   - So what?
   - Why should readers care?

**Template for Your NADES-SPME Work:**

```
[Background] Traditional extraction methods for pharmaceutical residues 
in water rely on toxic organic solvents and generate significant waste. 
Natural deep eutectic solvents (NADES) offer green alternatives but have 
limited application in solid phase microextraction (SPME). 

[Objective] This work developed a choline chloride-glucose NADES-coated 
SPME fiber for extraction of six pharmaceutical residues from water samples.

[Methods] NADES composition was optimized using experimental design, and 
the fiber was characterized by SEM and FTIR. Extraction conditions were 
optimized, and the method was validated according to ICH Q2 guidelines.

[Results] The method achieved detection limits of 0.05-0.15 ng/mL for 
target pharmaceuticals with extraction recoveries of 85-105% and relative 
standard deviations below 7%. Compared to commercial PDMS fibers, the 
NADES coating provided 2-3 fold higher extraction efficiency while 
eliminating organic solvent use. The fiber showed excellent stability 
over 100 extraction cycles with minimal performance degradation.

[Conclusions] This green SPME method offers a sustainable alternative for 
pharmaceutical monitoring in environmental waters, demonstrating that 
NADES can serve as effective and environmentally friendly SPME coatings.
```

### **3. INTRODUCTION**

**Purpose:** Convince reviewers and readers that your work is important and novel

**Structure (2-3 pages):**

**Paragraph 1: The Big Picture**
- Why does this field matter?
- What's the broad problem?
- Who cares and why?

*Example:*
"Pharmaceutical contamination of water bodies poses emerging environmental 
and health risks, with residues from antibiotics, hormones, and analgesics 
detected in surface waters worldwide at ng/L to Œºg/L levels..."

**Paragraph 2-3: Current Approaches and Limitations**
- What are existing methods?
- What are their problems?
- Use recent references (last 5 years)

*Example:*
"Current extraction methods for pharmaceuticals in water include solid 
phase extraction (SPE) [ref], liquid-liquid extraction (LLE) [ref], and 
solid phase microextraction (SPME) [ref]. While SPME offers advantages 
of solvent-free extraction and in situ sampling [ref], traditional fiber 
coatings such as polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) and polyacrylate (PA) show 
limited selectivity for polar pharmaceuticals [ref]. Moreover, commercial 
SPME fibers rely on synthetic polymers with environmental persistence..."

**Paragraph 4: The Gap**
- What's missing?
- What would solve the problem?

*Example:*
"Natural deep eutectic solvents (NADES) have emerged as green alternatives 
to organic solvents, formed by hydrogen bond interactions between natural 
components such as sugars, acids, and quaternary ammonium salts [refs]. 
Despite promising solvent properties, NADES application in SPME coatings 
remains largely unexplored, with only [X] previous reports [refs]..."

**Paragraph 5: Your Solution**
- What did you do?
- How is it novel?
- Why is it better?

*Example:*
"This work addresses this gap by developing NADES-coated SPME fibers 
specifically for pharmaceutical analysis in water. Using choline chloride-
glucose NADES immobilized on stainless steel wire through [method], we 
demonstrate [key advantages]. This represents the first application of 
NADES-SPME to pharmaceutical residue monitoring and expands green chemistry 
principles to microextraction techniques..."

**Final Paragraph: Study Objectives**
- Clear statement of what you did
- Bullet points acceptable

*Example:*
"The specific objectives were to: (1) synthesize and characterize choline 
chloride-glucose NADES with optimal viscosity and extraction properties, 
(2) develop NADES-coated SPME fibers and optimize extraction parameters, 
(3) validate the method for six pharmaceutical residues according to ICH 
guidelines, and (4) compare performance to commercial SPME fibers and 
traditional extraction methods."

### **4. MATERIALS AND METHODS**

**Purpose:** Enable replication, demonstrate rigor

**Organization:**

**4.1 Chemicals and Materials**
- List all reagents with purities and suppliers
- List all equipment with model numbers
- Standards and their sources

**4.2 NADES Preparation**
- Exact protocol
- Characterization methods

**4.3 SPME Fiber Fabrication**
- Step-by-step procedure
- Coating method
- Quality control

**4.4 Sample Preparation**
- Water sample collection
- Storage conditions
- Pre-treatment if any

**4.5 SPME Procedure**
- Extraction conditions (time, temp, agitation, pH, salt)
- Desorption conditions
- Instrumental analysis

**4.6 Instrumentation**
- LC-MS/MS or GC-MS conditions
- Column, mobile phase, gradients
- MS parameters

**4.7 Method Validation**
- Linearity, LOD, LOQ
- Precision (intra- and inter-day)
- Accuracy (recovery studies)
- Stability
- Matrix effects

**4.8 Data Analysis**
- Software used
- Statistical tests
- Optimization approach (e.g., response surface)

**Writing Style:**
- Past tense, passive voice acceptable
- Be specific (not "samples were heated" but "samples were heated at 60¬∞C for 30 min")
- Include all details needed to replicate
- If lengthy, consider Supporting Information

### **5. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION**

**Purpose:** Present findings and interpret significance

**Organization (Suggested Order):**

**5.1 NADES Characterization**
- Preparation and appearance
- Viscosity, density, water content
- FTIR or NMR confirmation
- Any thermal analysis

**5.2 SPME Fiber Characterization**
- SEM images showing morphology
- Coating thickness
- Uniformity assessment

**5.3 Optimization of Extraction Parameters**
- One variable at a time OR design of experiments
- Show graphs/figures for each variable
- Justify optimal conditions selected

**Key Variables for SPME:**
- Extraction time (equilibrium vs kinetic)
- Extraction temperature
- Sample pH
- Ionic strength (salting-out effect)
- Sample volume
- Desorption time and temperature

**5.4 Method Validation Results**
- Present as Table
- Linearity (R¬≤ values, range)
- LOD and LOQ values
- Precision (%RSD)
- Accuracy (% recovery)
- Compare to literature

**5.5 Comparison with Other Methods**
- vs. commercial SPME fibers
- vs. SPE
- vs. LLE
- Green metrics (solvents used, waste, time, cost)

**5.6 Real Sample Analysis**
- Environmental water samples
- Spiked recoveries
- Matrix effects addressed
- Practical applicability

**Writing Tips:**
- Start each subsection with purpose
- Present data in figures/tables (don't repeat in text)
- Interpret, don't just describe
- Compare to literature
- Acknowledge limitations
- Link back to objectives

---

### **6. CONCLUSIONS**

**Purpose:** Summarize key findings and significance

**Structure (0.5-1 page):**

**Paragraph 1: What You Did and Found**
- Restate objective briefly
- Key findings (numerical results)

**Paragraph 2: Significance and Advantages**
- Why it matters
- How it advances the field
- Green chemistry benefits quantified

**Paragraph 3: Future Directions** (optional)
- What's next?
- Potential applications
- Further research needed

**Avoid:**
- Introducing new information
- Over-claiming
- Repeating abstract verbatim

**Example Conclusion:**

"This work developed and validated a green SPME method using choline 
chloride-glucose NADES coating for determination of pharmaceutical residues 
in environmental waters. The method achieved detection limits of 0.05-0.15 
ng/mL with excellent precision (RSD < 7%) and accuracy (85-105% recovery), 
meeting regulatory requirements for environmental monitoring.

The NADES-SPME approach offers significant green chemistry advantages over 
traditional methods, eliminating organic solvent use entirely while providing 
2-3 fold improved extraction efficiency compared to commercial PDMS fibers. 
Over 100 extraction cycles, the fiber maintained stable performance, 
demonstrating practical viability for routine analysis.

This work expands both NADES applications and green SPME methodologies, 
demonstrating that naturally-derived solvents can serve as effective 
extraction phases. Future work will explore NADES diversity for broader 
analyte coverage and application to complex matrices such as wastewater 
and biological fluids."

## üìä FIGURES AND TABLES

### **Essential Figures:**

**Figure 1:** Schematic of NADES-SPME procedure
- Shows concept clearly
- Highlights green aspect

**Figure 2:** NADES characterization
- FTIR spectra, NMR, or other characterization
- Maybe include chemical structure

**Figure 3:** SEM images of fiber
- Before and after coating
- Shows morphology

**Figure 4:** Optimization results
- Response surface plots OR
- Bar charts for each variable

**Figure 5:** Chromatograms
- Blank, standard, spiked sample
- Shows peak resolution

**Figure 6:** Comparison with other methods
- Bar chart or spider diagram
- Green metrics comparison

**Figure 7:** Real sample analysis
- Results from environmental waters
- Demonstrates applicability

### **Essential Tables:**

**Table 1:** NADES composition and properties

**Table 2:** LC-MS/MS or instrumental parameters

**Table 3:** Method validation results
- Linearity, LOD, LOQ, precision, accuracy

**Table 4:** Comparison with literature methods
- Your method vs. others

**Table 5:** Real sample analysis results

### **Figure/Table Guidelines:**
- High resolution (300+ dpi for submission)
- Clear axis labels with units
- Legible fonts (Arial 8-10 pt minimum)
- Color figures acceptable (most journals)
- Referenced in text in order
- Self-explanatory captions

## üìö REFERENCES

### **Reference Management:**

**Use Reference Manager:**
- Mendeley (free, recommended)
- Zotero (free)
- EndNote (if university provides)

**Citation Style:**
- ACS style for Analytical Chemistry
- RSC style for Green Chemistry
- Journal-specific otherwise

**How Many References:**
- Introduction: 25-40 references
- Methods: 5-15 references
- Results/Discussion: 15-30 references
- Total: 40-70 typical for full paper

### **What to Cite:**

**Always Cite:**
- Methods you used (original papers)
- Instruments/commercial products (if appropriate)
- Data you reference (literature values)
- Concepts/theories
- Comparison methods

**Strategic Citing:**
- Recent papers (last 5 years) preferred
- High-impact journals
- Review articles for background
- Relevant work from target journal (shows you read it!)

### **Self-Citation:**
- Appropriate to cite your previous work
- Don't overdo it (excessive self-citation obvious)
- Cite only when truly relevant

## ‚è±Ô∏è WRITING TIMELINE

**Realistic Timeline for Full Paper:**

**Week 1-2: Outline and Figures**
- Create all figures and tables
- Draft outline with section headers
- Organize data

**Week 3-4: First Draft**
- Write Materials & Methods (easiest, start here)
- Write Results & Discussion
- Write Introduction
- Write Abstract and Conclusions last

**Week 5: Internal Review**
- Self-edit for clarity, flow
- Check all references
- Verify all data accurate

**Week 6: Co-Author Review**
- Send to co-authors (Dr. Nur, Prof. Goh, etc.)
- Incorporate feedback
- Resolve any disagreements

**Week 7: Final Polish**
- Professional editing (if available)
- Format to journal requirements
- Prepare cover letter
- Prepare supporting information

**Week 8: Submit!**

**Total: ~8 weeks for high-quality paper**

(This is realistic - rushing produces poor papers that get rejected)
